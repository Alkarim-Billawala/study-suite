Adrenal

Endo II (+ CAH from Endo III) · ~2 pp
trigger → answer
Physiology — "salt, sugar, sex; the deeper you go, the sweeter it gets"
Cortex zones (outer→inner): Glomerulosa → aldosterone (mineralocorticoid); Fasciculata → cortisol (glucocorticoid); Reticularis → androgens (DHEA). Medulla → catecholamines (chromaffin cells).
HPA axis: CRH → ACTH (cleaved from POMC) → cortisol; cortisol feeds back to suppress. Diurnal: peak AM, trough midnight (basis of late-night salivary test).
POMC link: high ACTH co-produces MSH → hyperpigmentation (primary adrenal insufficiency, ectopic ACTH, Nelson).
RAAS: renin (JG cells sense ↓BP/Na) → AngI →(ACE)→ AngII → vasoconstriction + aldosterone. Aldosterone acts on collecting-duct principal cells (ENaC): ↑Na in, ↑K & H⁺ out → excess = HTN + ↓K + metabolic alkalosis.
Aldosterone control = RAAS + K⁺, NOT ACTH — why it's preserved in secondary adrenal insufficiency.
Cortisol does: gluconeogenesis/insulin resistance, catabolism, immunosuppression, permissive for catecholamine vascular tone (why its loss → refractory shock), ↑appetite.

★ Adrenal emergencies
Adrenal (Addisonian) crisis — hypotension/shock refractory to fluids, ↓Na, ↑K, hypoglycemia, abdominal pain, fever. Triggers: infection, surgery, abrupt steroid withdrawal, starting levothyroxine in unrecognized AI. Tx: IV hydrocortisone 100 mg + aggressive isotonic saline (+ dextrose) + treat trigger. If undiagnosed, give dexamethasone (doesn't interfere with the cosyntropin test) and draw cortisol/ACTH — but never delay steroids to test.
Pheochromocytoma / catecholamine crisis — severe paroxysmal HTN, arrhythmia, end-organ ischemia. Never give a β-blocker first (unopposed α → crisis). Acute control with phentolamine or nitroprusside; definitive prep is α-blockade then β-blockade then resection.

Cushing syndrome

Features central obesity, moon face, buffalo hump, wide purple striae, proximal myopathy, easy bruising, HTN, hyperglycemia, osteoporosis, ↓K alkalosis, mood change
#1 cause overall exogenous glucocorticoids (ask about steroids; ACTH suppressed, no adrenal mass)
Two-step work-up
① Confirm excess (2 of 3) 24-h urine free cortisol · late-night salivary cortisol · overnight 1-mg dexamethasone (fails to suppress)
② Localize with ACTH low → adrenal CT (adenoma/carcinoma) · high/normal → ACTH-dependent → pituitary MRI, high-dose dex (pituitary suppresses, ectopic doesn't), ± inferior petrosal sinus sampling
Treatment surgery (transsphenoidal / adrenalectomy / resect ectopic); steroidogenesis inhibitors (ketoconazole, metyrapone, osilodrostat) as bridge
Nelson syndrome pituitary corticotroph tumor enlargement after bilateral adrenalectomy

Adrenal insufficiency

Primary (Addison) cortex destroyed → ↓cortisol + ↓aldosterone + ↓androgens; ↑ACTH → hyperpigmentation; ↓Na, ↑K, hypoglycemia
Primary causes autoimmune (#1 developed world, ± APS), TB (#1 worldwide), adrenal hemorrhage (Waterhouse-Friderichsen), metastases, adrenoleukodystrophy
Secondary ↓ACTH (pituitary) / tertiary ↓CRH (chronic steroid withdrawal = commonest); aldosterone intact → no ↑K, no pigment; mild ↓Na possible
Diagnosis & treatment
Diagnose low AM cortisol; ACTH (↑ primary, ↓ secondary); cosyntropin (ACTH) stim — no cortisol rise = primary
Replace hydrocortisone + fludrocortisone (mineralocorticoid — primary only)
Sick-day rules double/triple glucocorticoid in illness; injectable for vomiting; medical-alert ID
⚠ don't start levothyroxine before glucocorticoid — precipitates crisis

Primary aldosteronism (Conn)

Why it matters commonest cause of secondary HTN; bilateral adrenal hyperplasia or unilateral adenoma
Suspect resistant HTN, HTN + hypokalemia (many are normokalemic), HTN + adrenal incidentaloma, young HTN
Labs HTN, hypokalemia, metabolic alkalosis
Screen aldosterone:renin ratio (↑aldo, suppressed renin)
Confirm saline-suppression/salt-loading (aldo fails to suppress)
Lateralize adrenal vein sampling: unilateral adenoma → adrenalectomy; bilateral hyperplasia → MRA (spironolactone/eplerenone)

Pheochromocytoma

What catecholamine-secreting medullary tumor (chromaffin cells); paraganglioma if extra-adrenal
Classic triad + HTN episodic headache, palpitations, diaphoresis; sustained or paroxysmal hypertension, pallor, anxiety
"Rule of 10s" ~10% bilateral, extra-adrenal, malignant; familial fraction is high
Genetics MEN2 (RET), VHL, NF1, SDHx — screen young/familial cases
Diagnose plasma free metanephrines or 24-h urine fractionated metanephrines → then CT/MRI (± MIBG)
Manage α-block first (phenoxybenzamine/doxazosin) ×10–14 d + volume expansion → then β-block → resection

Congenital adrenal hyperplasia

21-hydroxylase deficiency (90%) can't make cortisol/aldosterone → ACTH drive shunts precursors to androgens; screen with ↑17-OHP
Classic salt-wasting neonatal crisis: ↓Na, ↑K, hypotension; ambiguous genitalia in 46,XX
Non-classic (late-onset) hirsutism, acne, oligomenorrhea — PCOS mimic; morning 17-OHP → ACTH-stim to confirm
11β-hydroxylase def HTN (deoxycorticosterone) + virilization
17α-hydroxylase def HTN + ↓K, no androgens (absent secondary sexual development)
Treat glucocorticoid (suppress ACTH) ± mineralocorticoid (salt-wasting)

Incidentaloma & exam pitfalls

Adrenal incidentaloma
Two questions functioning? (1-mg dex for cortisol, plasma metanephrines, ARR if HTN/↓K) and malignant? (size >4 cm, >10 HU non-contrast, irregular, growth)
Most are non-functioning benign adenomas → observe; resect if functioning or suspicious/>4 cm
Pitfalls
Exclude pheo first before ANY adrenal surgery or biopsy
α before β in pheochromocytoma, always
Secondary AI no hyperkalemia, no hyperpigmentation (aldosterone preserved)
Treat crisis before testing dexamethasone preserves the cosyntropin test
Steroid taper chronic glucocorticoids suppress HPA — never stop abruptly

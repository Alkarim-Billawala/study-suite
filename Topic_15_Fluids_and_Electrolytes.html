Fluids & Electrolytes

KU 2 · ~2 pp
trigger → answer
The concept that unlocks sodium problems
Sodium content = ECF volume (edema vs dehydration). Sodium concentration = water balance / cell size. They're independent — a patient can be volume-overloaded and hyponatremic at once.
Dysnatremias are water problems: hyponatremia = relative water excess; hypernatremia = water deficit. ADH (driven by osmolality and by low effective volume) is the central lever.
Correct slowly: chronic hyponatremia too fast → osmotic demyelination; hypernatremia too fast → cerebral edema. Target ≤8–10 mmol/L/24 h.
Refractory K⁺ or Ca²⁺? Check and replace magnesium.
★ Electrolyte emergencies
Severe symptomatic hyponatremia (seizure, coma) — 3% hypertonic saline bolus to raise Na a few mmol/L acutely; otherwise correct slowly to avoid osmotic demyelination.
Severe hyperkalemia + ECG changes — IV calcium gluconate first (stabilize) → insulin/dextrose + salbutamol (shift) → furosemide/binder/dialysis (excrete). ECG: peaked T → wide QRS → sine wave.

Hyponatremia — the algorithm

① Osmolality normal/high → pseudohyponatremia (lipids/paraproteins) or hyperglycemia (translocational; correct Na +~2.4/5.5 mmol glucose), mannitol
② If hypotonic → volume
Hypovolemic renal (diuretics, salt-wasting, adrenal — UNa>20) vs extrarenal (GI, third-space — UNa<20) → isotonic saline
Euvolemic SIADH, hypothyroid, glucocorticoid deficiency, psychogenic polydipsia/beer potomania (dilute urine) → fluid restriction
Hypervolemic CHF, cirrhosis, nephrotic, renal failure (↓effective volume → ADH) → salt + water restriction, treat cause

SIADH

Criteria euvolemic hypotonic hyponatremia, urine osm >100 (inappropriately concentrated), UNa >30, low uric acid, normal thyroid/adrenal
Causes CNS (stroke, tumor), pulmonary (pneumonia, SCLC), drugs (SSRIs, carbamazepine), pain/post-op, malignancy
Treat fluid restriction; salt ± loop; vaptans/demeclocycline for refractory

Hypernatremia

Mechanism water deficit — thirst normally protects, so occurs in those without water access (elderly, infants, AMS) or with DI
Causes pure water loss (insensible, central/nephrogenic DI), hypotonic loss (GI, osmotic diuresis), rarely Na gain
Treat free water (oral/NG or IV D5W); correct slowly; address DI (desmopressin if central)

Potassium

Hyperkalemia
Causes ↓excretion (renal failure, ACEi/ARB, K-sparing, type 4 RTA/hypoaldo, Addison), shift (acidosis, insulin deficiency, cell lysis/rhabdo/tumor lysis, β-blocker, digoxin), pseudo (hemolyzed sample)
ECG peaked T → ↑PR/↓P → wide QRS → sine → VF
Hypokalemia
Causes GI loss, renal (diuretics, hyperaldo, Bartter/Gitelman, type 1/2 RTA, Mg deficiency), shift (alkalosis, insulin, β-agonist, refeeding)
ECG flat T, U waves, ST depression
Treat KCl; correct Mg (refractory hypoK)

Mg · PO₄ · pitfalls

Hypomagnesemia diuretics, PPI, alcohol, diarrhea → refractory hypoK/hypoCa, torsades → replace Mg
Hypermagnesemia renal failure/antacids → ↓reflexes, resp depression → Ca gluconate
Hypophosphatemia refeeding, DKA treatment, alcohol → weakness, rhabdo, hemolysis
Pitfalls
Correct slowly chronic hypoNa (ODS); hyperNa (cerebral edema)
Hyperglycemia → pseudohyponatremia; correct the Na
Vomiting → hypokalemia mainly via renal loss (bicarbonaturia + aldosterone), not the vomitus
RTA & K type 4 → hyperK; types 1/2 → hypoK

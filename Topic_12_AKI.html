Acute Kidney Injury

KU 1 · ~2 pp
trigger → answer
Definition & the pre/intra/post framework
AKI (KDIGO): ↑creatinine ≥26.5 µmol/L in 48 h, or ≥1.5× baseline within 7 d, or urine output <0.5 mL/kg/h for ≥6 h.
Three buckets: prerenal (hypoperfusion, reversible), intrinsic (ATN/AIN/glomerular/vascular), postrenal (obstruction). Always get a renal ultrasound + bladder scan to exclude obstruction, and review the med list.
Urine tells the story: bland = prerenal/postrenal; muddy-brown granular casts = ATN; WBC casts = AIN; RBC casts = glomerular.
FENa: <1% prerenal, >1–2% ATN — but unreliable on diuretics (use FEurea <35%), and low in contrast/glomerular/early obstruction.
★ AKI emergencies
Hyperkalemia with ECG changes — IV calcium gluconate first (membrane stabilization) → insulin/dextrose + salbutamol (shift) → saline/furosemide or binder (excrete) → dialysis if refractory.
Dialyze for AEIOU — Acidosis (refractory pH<7.1), Electrolytes (refractory hyperkalemia), Intoxication (methanol, ethylene glycol, lithium, salicylates), Overload (refractory pulmonary edema), Uremia (pericarditis, encephalopathy, bleeding).

Prerenal

Causes true volume loss (hemorrhage, GI, diuretics); ↓effective volume (CHF, cirrhosis, sepsis); hemodynamic — NSAIDs (afferent constriction), ACEi/ARB (efferent dilation), hepatorenal
Labs FENa <1%, UNa <20, urine osm >500, BUN:Cr >20, bland sediment
Key feature reverses with restoration of perfusion; prolonged → ATN

Intrinsic — ATN & AIN

ATN — ischemic prolonged prerenal/shock
ATN — nephrotoxic contrast, aminoglycosides, amphotericin, cisplatin; myoglobin (rhabdo), hemoglobin, urate (tumor lysis), myeloma casts
ATN clues muddy-brown granular casts, FENa >1–2%; phases oliguric → polyuric recovery
AIN drugs (NSAIDs, PPIs, β-lactams, sulfa, allopurinol) ~7–10 d later; fever/rash/eosinophilia (triad often absent); WBC casts, sterile pyuria → stop drug ± steroids
Vascular TTP/HUS, scleroderma renal crisis, cholesterol emboli (post-cath, livedo, blue toes, eosinophilia), malignant HTN

Postrenal (obstruction)

Causes BPH, bilateral stones, pelvic malignancy, retroperitoneal fibrosis, neurogenic bladder (needs bilateral or solitary-kidney obstruction)
Diagnose renal US → hydronephrosis; bladder scan
Relieve outlet (BPH) → urethral catheter; ureteric → stent/nephrostomy
Watch post-obstructive diuresis — monitor fluids/electrolytes

Work-up & complications

Work-up urinalysis + microscopy (casts), FENa/FEurea, renal US, bladder scan, med review, volume assessment, CK if rhabdo suspected
Rhabdomyolysis ↑CK, dipstick blood+ but no RBCs (myoglobin), ↑K/PO₄, ↓Ca → aggressive fluids
Complications
Watch for hyperkalemia, metabolic acidosis, volume overload, hyperphosphatemia, uremia (pericarditis, encephalopathy, platelet dysfunction)

Management & pitfalls

General treat the cause, optimize perfusion/volume, stop nephrotoxins, renally dose drugs, manage K⁺/acidosis
Contrast nephropathy prevent with hydration; distinguish from cholesterol emboli (delayed, eosinophilia)
Pitfalls
FENa on diuretics use FEurea instead
Anuric AKI eGFR ≈ 0 — equations invalid in non-steady state
Triple whammy NSAID + ACEi/ARB + diuretic → prerenal AKI
Always exclude obstruction US/bladder scan early
